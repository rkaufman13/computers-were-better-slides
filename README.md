# Computers were better when they were worse
     
 ## A manifesto


  **Ever notice how there are only 3 websites anymore?**

  And they all just want to sell you things or take your attention?
  
  I am not nostalgic for all of the old Internet.

  But the cool thing about the "old Internet" is that if you visited a site, you could be reasonably sure that the content on it was created by a person, and if the person was trying to sell you something, you would know, because they would say "please buy this thing that I am selling."

  The lines are a lot more blurry now, and we all lose.

  Doomscrolling, surveillance capitalism, the "dead Internet..." it's all part of the same thing, just making billionaires richer.

  But you can do something about this! Actually 3 things.


### Thing one(1): Participate in the smallweb

  The smallweb is made up of pages that are simple, don't require tracking or cookies, and are made by indie creators.

  The web has users, the smallweb has people.

  "The web is also a creative and cultural space that need not confine itself to the conventions defined by commercial product design and marketing." -- https://smallweb.page/why

  One way to participate in the smallweb is to just read independent blogs, and hopefully share the things you like reading.

  I like discovering new reads through https://bubbles.town. There's also https://indieblog.page, which works like StumbleUpon used to, and https://blogofthe.day, which just shows you a random blog, which changes every day.

  Join [Mastodon](https://instances.social/) (it's like twitter for weird people). Not only are all the weirdos here, but there's no "algorithm." You follow the people you follow, and when you get to the end, you're done. I kinda love this.

  Use an RSS reader like [Feedly](https://feedly.com) or [The Old Reader](https://theoldreader.com). RSS is free, it's NOT dead (please stop saying this), and is a wonderful way to keep up with your favorite writers or creators even if they don't have a newsletter or post on socials.

  > Side note: The RSS syndication protocol that these readers use is an open standard that's been around forever, and it's the same open standard that podcast syndication uses. That's why you can listen to your favorite podcast "on Apple, Spotify, or wherever you get your podcasts" and aren't locked into a specific app. 

  
###  Thing 2 (two): Use fewer US-based services (this sucks and is hard sorry)
  
  Some easy switches

| Gmail   | Google  | Chrome  |
|---------|---------|-----------------------|
| Proton Mail  | Qwant or Kagi   | Vivaldi  |

Mostly this means degoogling here - partly because Google has a monopoly on everything, but also because if your email is physically stored outside the US, it is subject to different protections.

I've been using Proton Mail for about six months. It feels like a good substitute for Gmail at a reasonable price. Unfortunately, the rest of the Proton Suite hasn't yet evolved to where I feel comfortable switching from, say, Google Calendar.

Search is an interesting one because there are reasons you might want to switch away from Google that have nothing to do with privacy. The AI answers pushed out by default are pretty annoying, for one thing.

Qwant and Ecosia are based in the EU, and have various advantages and disadvantages. Kagi is _not_ based in the EU but works really well (unfortunately you do have to pay for it); the easiest switch is probably just to Duck Duck Go.

And then finally, you may want to consider switching browsers. People rave about Vivaldi; I've been trying Zen which is nice, and other folks enjoy Brave, although they have made some PR missteps (and usually recovered from them).

The important thing to remember is that none of this is perfect, and we shouldn't be aiming for perfection either. Pick one thing to try to switch out, try it, see how you like it. 

More here: https://disconnect.blog/getting-off-us-tech-a-guide/
  
### thing 3: social media is killing us

Things i saw on IG last time I went there:

  - a fitness influencer
  - cheese ad
  - standup comedian
  - gym comedian
  - gym ad (IG thinks I'm fat)
  - Jerry Saltz post (cool guy, not my friend tho)
  - a pretty good garfield parody (credit where credit is due)
  - a cute video of a baby quail
  - another ad
  - I have no idea what this was https://www.instagram.com/katelinten/p/DaNv2QyHHqk/
  - another ad
  - another comedy reel
  - at some point I disassociated and just kept scrolling but there were at least 5 more posts before
  - FINALLY a post from someone I know personally

Maybe this is not how I should be spending my time? This isn't a hot take anymore but why do we call it "social" media if we're just looking at strangers? If you are into "influencer media" that's fine. I personally love smoothing my brain with instagram now and then. But until I actually sat down and counted, did I understand how much of my feed was taken up by people I don't know, don't follow, and don't care about? If our friends aren't on social media anymore (or aren't findable), maybe we will have more fun and meaningful connections if we go where they go.
  
### thing 3.5:  Make content where you can keep/control it

  [Because sites keep shutting down and deleting your data.](https://indieweb.org/site-deaths)

  The other issue with social media is if we post on it, we're posting onto platforms that don't care about us and don't want you to get your content back out.
  
  Consider using sites/platforms that make it easy to leave. Easier said than done, especially if some of your 'personal brand' is based on having reach on social media.

  If you can't do that, then at least consider that you might want to grab copies of things you care about. [This digital scrapbook project](https://alexwlchan.net/2025/social-media-scrapbook/) is a wild inspiration.

  And if you can use POSSE (Publish On your own Site, Syndicate Elsewhere), that's even better. Consider that both Medium and dev.to allow you to hook up an RSS feed as an input source, so that you could publish on your own dot-com and then still reap the benefits of engaging with the community on those other sites.

  But really, what I'd like to inspire you to do is make content, period. Without caring about how many 'likes' it gets.

  Start a blog! Make a website! About anything you want! because computers are supposed to be FUN. If you do, please hit me up, I'll follow you.
